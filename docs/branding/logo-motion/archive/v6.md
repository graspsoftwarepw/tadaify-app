# tadaify — Logo Motion v6: The Step Forward

> *Zasada po polsku, jedno zdanie: twórca robi jeden spokojny krok do przodu na swojej scenie, zatrzymuje się na chwilę — „jestem tu" — i wraca do środka. Cała reszta pętli to cisza.*

---

## 1. Design principle

The motion vocabulary of v6 is **one gesture**: a single lateral step out from centre, a long deliberate hold, and an unhurried return home. That is the entire loop. Everything else — 9-plus seconds of every cycle — is stillness. The mark is static by default; the gesture happens rarely enough that a viewer who glances twice may wonder whether they imagined it the first time. That uncertainty is the design: it makes the viewer look again, and on the second look the metaphor clicks.

The choice of a *step-and-hold* rather than a *sway* is load-bearing. A sway (v5) is a pendulum — it implies no agency, just mechanics. A step implies a decision: the warm orb chose to move forward, paused there intentionally, and returned when ready. That is the vocabulary of a performer greeting their audience, which is exactly the metaphor the brand has locked. The hold is the greeting. The return is the confidence of someone who knows the stage is theirs and doesn't need to perform.

Sides alternate across cycles (right, then left, then right) so the motion is not handed — the brand has no dominant side, which matches a creator platform where every user is the star of their own page.

---

## 2. Timing table

Full cycle: **14.0 s**. Amplitude: **±6 SVG units** in a `viewBox="0 0 100 100"` system, where the large orb has `r=40`. That is **15 % of the large orb radius** — the warm orb never approaches the purple boundary.

| Keyframe | Time into cycle | Transform | Phase | Easing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 % | 0.0 s | `translate(0, 0)` | Cycle start (already at rest) | — |
| 7.1 % | 1.0 s | `translate(+6px, 0)` | End of *step out* | `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)` |
| 22.9 % | 3.2 s | `translate(+6px, 0)` | End of *hold* (2.2 s at displacement) | — (no interpolation: identical values) |
| 30.7 % | 4.3 s | `translate(0, 0)` | End of *return* | `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)` |
| 100 % | 14.0 s | `translate(0, 0)` | End of *long rest* (9.7 s still) | — |

Sides alternate via a two-circle trick (see §6): a right-stepping circle and a left-stepping circle are rendered at the same coordinates with opposite animation phase, each on a 28 s timeline offset by 14 s. At any moment only one is displaced; the other is at centre and visually subsumed.

The cubic-bezier curve `(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)` is the Material Design *standard easing* — deliberately not elastic, not bounced. It's the easing used for sheet transitions in iOS, Android, and Linear. That choice is where "premium" lives.

---

## 3. Why v1–v5 failed (independent analysis)

**v1 — four alternate patterns.** Failure mode was decision paralysis on the user's side, not motion design. Four directions, four feelings; the user wanted one point of view, not a menu. The lesson: a brand mark does not A/B test in the mockup phase.

**v2 — arc-sweep around 4 points.** The motion traced a continuous path around the inside of the purple orb. The eye read this as orbital: a satellite, a loading spinner, a cursor hunting for a target. The metaphor of "creator" was lost because orbital motion has no actor — a planet doesn't *choose* to orbit.

**v3 — pop-at-edge with bounce back.** The bounce is the tell. Bounce easing (`cubic-bezier` with overshoot or elastic springs) signals *cartoon*, *playful*, *fast*. Apple and Linear never use bounces on brand marks. The mark became a Figma demo.

**v4 — travel + wave at each of four edges.** The failure here was event density. Four visits × (travel + three-cycle rock + return) = twelve discrete visual events in ~12 s. The eye had no place to rest. Worse, waving at the viewer (a perpendicular rocking motion at each edge) reads as *attention-seeking*. A premium brand does not wave; it stands.

**v5 — subtle horizontal sway, 9 s cycle.** v5 fixed the over-motion of v4 but replaced it with mechanical regularity. Left, right, left, right — the pendulum. A pendulum has no intent. The viewer sees a clock, not a creator. v5 is the closest v1–v5 got to "calm", but calmness without intent is blank. The step-and-hold of v6 keeps the calm but adds intent.

---

## 4. Tradeoffs honestly named

**Asymmetry across short views.** A viewer who sees less than one full 28-second symmetric cycle (i.e. anyone on a landing page for <30 s) sees the logo step in one direction only. The brand gets a transient handedness. I judge this acceptable because (a) the static mark is perfectly symmetric and dominates surfaces like favicons, app icons, and email signatures; (b) the handedness resets on every page reload; (c) alternatives — stepping in both directions within a single 14 s cycle — collapse back into v5's pendulum.

**Reads as static next to loud motion.** On a landing page with a looping hero video, an autoplay carousel, or a stream of user-generated content, v6 will visually lose. That is the deliberate tradeoff of choosing 70 % stillness. The mark is not the entertainment; it's the anchor. If a specific product page needs a more visible mark, the CTA is to tone down the neighbouring motion, not the logo.

---

## 5. Accessibility

`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` collapses v6 to the static centred mark — zero motion, identical to the brand lock §5 static placement. Implementation is a single CSS rule (`animation: none !important` on both warm-orb elements) and requires no JavaScript.

The wordmark `tada!ify` never animates in any mode or size. At favicon scales (32 px, 16 px) the SVG ships without the animation class, because at 16 px a ±6-unit displacement is sub-pixel and would alias as jitter rather than motion.

---

## 6. Implementation hint

**Recommendation: pure CSS keyframes.** No Framer Motion. Rationale: zero runtime, zero JavaScript cost, deterministic across all browsers, respects `prefers-reduced-motion` natively, renders identically in SSR and in static HTML emails / OG-image generators. Framer Motion is warranted when motion responds to state (hover, scroll, gesture); this motion is ambient and needs no React machinery.

**The two-orb phase trick.** A naive single-circle keyframe that goes `center → right → center → left → center` collapses back into v5's pendulum — one continuous back-and-forth. To get the *alternating-cycle* feel (right on loop N, left on loop N+1), render two identical warm circles stacked at the same coordinates, each driven by a one-sided keyframe, with a half-period animation-delay between them. At any instant at most one circle is displaced; the other sits at centre and is fully occluded by the purple orb's visual weight.

```css
@keyframes orbStepRight {
  0%    { transform: translate(0, 0); }
  7.1%  { transform: translate(6px, 0); }   /* step out — 1.0 s */
  22.9% { transform: translate(6px, 0); }   /* hold — 2.2 s */
  30.7% { transform: translate(0, 0); }     /* return — 1.1 s */
  100%  { transform: translate(0, 0); }     /* rest — 9.7 s */
}
@keyframes orbStepLeft {
  0%    { transform: translate(0, 0); }
  7.1%  { transform: translate(-6px, 0); }
  22.9% { transform: translate(-6px, 0); }
  30.7% { transform: translate(0, 0); }
  100%  { transform: translate(0, 0); }
}

.orb-warm--r {
  animation: orbStepRight 28s cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1) infinite;
  transform-origin: 50px 50px; /* center of viewBox 0 0 100 100 */
}
.orb-warm--l {
  animation: orbStepLeft  28s cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1) infinite;
  animation-delay: -14s;       /* half-period offset → alternation */
  transform-origin: 50px 50px;
}

@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce) {
  .orb-warm--r, .orb-warm--l { animation: none !important; }
}
```

Markup — two concentric warm circles in the SVG, both `r=20` at `cx=50 cy=50`:

```html
<svg viewBox="0 0 100 100" role="img" aria-label="tadaify">
  <circle cx="50" cy="50" r="40" fill="url(#stageGrad)" />
  <circle class="orb-warm--r" cx="50" cy="50" r="20" fill="#F59E0B" />
  <circle class="orb-warm--l" cx="50" cy="50" r="20" fill="#F59E0B" />
</svg>
```

The 28 s timeline means the keyframe percentages are half of the numbers quoted in the timing table (which measures against the logical 14 s cycle). The visible gesture still lasts 4.3 s; the unseen "other-side twin" consumes the remaining 9.7 s before handing back.

---

## 7. One question back to you

**Should the gesture happen every 14 s (current) or every 20 s?**

- **14 s** — the gesture fires ~4 times per minute. A visitor on a landing hero will see at least one step within ~10 s (the typical above-the-fold dwell time). Legibility is strong; "did it just move?" is answered quickly.
- **20 s** — the gesture fires 3 times per minute. The rest phase stretches to ~15 s. The mark feels even more premium (closer to Apple's Liquid Retina wallpaper pace) but a visitor who bounces in under 15 s sees a fully static mark and may not encode the motion at all.

My recommendation is **14 s** — legibility on the first visit matters more than extra stillness, because most landing-page visits are short and first impressions are what the brand is selling. But this is the one lever where I want your call before shipping.
